That's an excellent question with a more complex answer than you might expect. There isn't a single, fixed date like the last day of school. The schedule is flexible and politically determined.

Here’s a breakdown of when and how Congress goes out of session.

The Short Answer

Congress typically aims to adjourn for the year sometime inmid-to-late December. However, the exact date changes every year and often gets pushed back due to last-minute negotiations on major legislation, like the federal budget.

---

The Detailed Explanation: Key Concepts

To understand the schedule, you need to know the difference between a "recess" and an "adjournment."

#### 1. Recess (Taking a Break) Arecessis a temporary break within a congressional session. The House and Senate take several of these throughout the year. During a recess, members often return to their home states and districts to meet with constituents.

Common recesses include:

  • State/District Work Periods:Several week-long breaks scheduled throughout the year (e.g., Presidents' Day, Memorial Day, July 4th).
  • August Recess:The longest break of the year, typically lasting four to five weeks.
  • Thanksgiving Recess:Usually a one-week break.

Even during a recess, Congress can be called back to Washington, D.C., to deal with an emergency.

#### 2. Adjournment (Ending the Session for the Year) This is what most people mean when they ask when Congress "goes out of session." The formal end of a session for the year is called anadjournmentsine die(a Latin term meaning "without a day"). This signifies that the session is over, and Congress will not meet again until the next session begins in January.

  • A "Congress" (e.g., the 118th Congress) lasts for two years.
  • Each two-year term is divided into twosessions, each lasting about one year.
The adjournmentsine die* in December marks the end of the first or second session of that Congress.

The Typical Congressional Calendar Rhythm

While the exact dates are set by the Majority Leaders in the House and Senate, a typical year looks like this:

  • January:A new session begins.
  • February - July:In session, with several one- or two-week recesses for holidays and district work.
  • August:The long August recess.
  • September - October:A busy period focused on passing spending bills before the fiscal year ends on September 30th.
  • November (Election Year):Members typically go on a long recess before the November election to campaign. After the election, they return for a"lame-duck" session, where they try to pass legislation before the new Congress is sworn in.
December:A final, intense push to pass any remaining critical legislation, followed by the adjournmentsine die*.

The most accurate information comes directly from Congress itself. These calendars are set at the beginning of the year but are always subject to change.
