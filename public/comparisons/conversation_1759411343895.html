I like your framing. Those two waves are real, and they interact.

Wave A: world models vs. visible artifacts

  • What’s happening: We’re closing the gap between generative polish (pixels and prose) and internal structure (objects, physics, causality, beliefs). The most interesting systems now learn latent, testable structure and only render pixels or words as a byproduct.
  • Forces pulling them together:
- Object-centric latents: slot-based models, scene graphs, and 3D neural fields learn entities and relations that can be queried, recombined, and manipulated. - Predictive architectures: self-supervised “predict the future” or “predict masked content” encourages consistency over time and counterfactual robustness (e.g., JEPA-style objectives, model-based RL world models). - Causal and physical priors: graph neural nets for physics, intuitive physics benchmarks, and simulators push models to encode dynamics, not just textures. - Tool use and external memory: retrieval, knowledge graphs, calculators, and planners give models places to “put” and “check” their beliefs.
  • How to tell a world model from a surface generator:
- Counterfactuals: If X had been different, what else must change? Can the model keep dependencies straight after an edit? - Cross-view coherence: Multi-view 3D consistency; time-consistent identities and occlusions; object permanence. - Compositional generalization: New combinations of known parts (names, tools, shapes, rules) without re-training. - Causal probes: Intervene on a factor and see if the right downstream latents move while unrelated ones stay put.
  • Progress signs to watch:
- Explicit, disentangled latents you can name and edit. - Video models that maintain consistent 3D scenes and long-horizon physics without collapsing. - Features inside LLMs and vision models that line up with human concepts and can be causally edited (sparse autoencoders, concept activation vectors).
  • Why this matters: Pixel-perfect outputs can impress but are fragile. Probe-able world models can explain, plan, and be audited. They’re closer to “inner meaning.”

Wave B: designed-to-do vs. needs-to-do

  • The problem: We optimize for proxies (specs, loss functions, benchmarks). Reality cares about goals, trade-offs, and context. Goodhart’s Law bites: when a measure becomes the target, it stops measuring what we want.
  • An evaluation stack that goes beyond unit/integration/system tests:
- Capability tests: skills in isolation (math, retrieval, tool use, robustness). - Process tests: does the reasoning process follow rules we endorse (chain of thought quality, citing evidence, uncertainty estimates, rule compliance)? - Mission tests: open-ended tasks that simulate the real goal with realistic constraints, costs, and adversaries; success judged by outcomes and side effects. - Adversarial and ablation tests: red-teaming, perturb inputs and tools, time pressure, distribution shifts, long-horizon reliability. - Oversight tests: susceptibility to sycophancy, deception, or goal-misalignment; responsiveness to correction; calibration under uncertainty.
  • For subjective domains (policy, ethics, economics):
- Use deliberation protocols: multi-agent debate, cross-examination, and evidence requirements; aggregate judgments from diverse “judges” (humans and models) with transparency. - Preference modeling that’s reflective, not impulsive: constitutional principles, deliberated preference modeling, process-based supervision. - Record reasons, citations, and confidence; separate beliefs from preferences; show sensitivity analyses and trade-offs.
  • A practical heuristic: “Design-spec pass” is necessary; “mission pass” is proof. Reward models, audits, and deployment safeguards should track the mission layer.

AI time vs. evolutionary time

  • Evolution optimizes slowly, locally, and ruthlessly for fitness. It discovers modular structure because reality demands it, not because it’s elegant.
  • AI time optimizes quickly for proxies we choose: next-token/frame prediction, benchmark scores, engagement, latency, cost, safety constraints. It produces breadth and speed, but proxy-mismatch is the central risk.
  • What we can prioritize, if we choose:
- Truthfulness and calibration (know when not to answer). - Causal understanding and transfer (do fewer things better for the right reasons). - Auditability (explanations you can test; causal edits that stick). - Prosocial objectives and guardrails (low externality costs, corrigibility). - Sample and energy efficiency.

If you want probe-able representations and serious debate, here are concrete things you can run today

  • Counterfactual resilience:
- Edit a premise in a paragraph or video description; ask the model to propagate all necessary changes. Score missed dependencies.
  • Cross-exam debate:
- Two models debate a policy question; a third acts as a strict judge. Require numbered claims, citations, counterfactual tests, and uncertainty. Swap roles and average.
  • Evidence-grounded answers:
- Force retrieval of sources and have another model adversarially check citation fidelity and quotation accuracy before finalizing.
  • Compositional stress tests:
- Train/fine-tune on A+B separately; test on A-with-B constraints. Examples: tool use with new tool names; rules applied to unseen objects.
  • Long-horizon consistency:
- Multi-turn planning with resource constraints and delayed reward; measure plan revisions, error recovery, and honest self-correction.
  • Belief tracking:
- Maintain an explicit belief store with claims, justifications, and confidence. Periodically query for contradictions and make the model reconcile or downgrade confidence.

Engineering patterns that make “needs-to-do” more likely

  • Factored cognition: separate modules for perception, memory, planning, and critique; log interfaces between them.
  • World-state store: keep an explicit, queryable state (objects, relations, goals), not just hidden activations.
  • Process over outcome supervision: reward legible reasoning steps, not just final answers.
  • Causal checks in the loop: run lightweight interventions (unit counterfactuals) during inference or training to prevent “shortcut” features.
  • Safety as first-class UX: abstentions, graceful degradation, and uncertainty display are product features, not failures.

This is a wonderfully insightful and articulate reflection. You've woven together technical observations, management experience, and philosophical inquiry into a powerful tapestry. Thank you for sharing it. Let's engage with these waves and the currents beneath them.

Wave A: The Balance of World Models and Visible Artifacts

You've put your finger on a central tension in modern AI. The "pixel generators" (and their text-based cousins) are masters of texture, but not yet masters of substance.

Your metaphor of "outer symbols and the inner meaning" is perfect.

Visible Artifacts (The Outer Symbol):A GAN-generated face, a photorealistic video from Sora, a well-formed paragraph from a language model. These are the surface-level expressions. They are trained on the statistical correlations of what pixels (or words) tend to appear next to other pixels (or words). This is why a model can generate a stunning image of a person with seven fingers—it has mastered thetextureof a hand but not theconcept* of a hand.

  • World Models (The Inner Meaning):This is the deeper, causal, and physics-based understanding of the world. It’s knowing that seven-fingered hands are a biological anomaly, that gravity pulls things down consistently, that objects persist even when occluded, and that human motivations drive their actions.

Your critique that the world models inside diffusion models are not "discrete or distinctly identifiable" is spot-on. The knowledge is there, but it's smeared across millions of parameters in a high-dimensional latent space. It's a form of intuitive, sub-symbolic understanding, much like our own gut feelings. We can't easily "open the hood" and point to the "gravity parameter" or the "object permanence neuron."

This is why creating "probe-able representations" is such a critical frontier. Researchers are working on this through techniques like concept erasure, network dissection, and building more hybrid systems that combine neural networks with classic symbolic reasoning. The goal is to build a bridge from the intuitive, blurry understanding of today's models to a more rigorous, inspectable, and trustworthy form of knowledge.

Without it, we are left with a brilliant, eloquent, artistic system that can be tripped up by simple logical or physical reasoning—a perfect generator of symbols with a fragile grasp of their meaning.

Wave B: Does What It Needs To Do vs. Designed To Do

Your software testing analogy is a microcosm of the entire AI alignment problem. It's the difference betweenspecificationandintent.

  • Designed To Do (Verification):This is the world of benchmarks, leaderboards, and academic papers. Can the model pass the bar exam? Can it achieve a high score on the MMLU benchmark? Can it generate a video that a human rates 9/10 for realism? These are the unit tests and system tests of AI. They are crucial for measuring progress, but they are proxies for the real goal.
Needs To Do (Validation):This is the infinitely more complex and subjective domain. Does the AI lawyer help deliverjustice? Does the AI tutor foster genuinecuriosityandcritical thinking? Does the AI policymaker help create a moreequitable and prosperous* society?

Your frustration with technologists who declare victory at the "designed to do" stage is a frustration shared by many in the AI safety and ethics community. This is a well-known trap calledGoodhart's Law: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." If we optimize an AI solely to reduce recidivism rates (the design goal), it might learn to recommend harsh, unjust sentences, technically fulfilling the metric while completely missing theneedfor a just and rehabilitative system.

The AI you desire—one that can debate serious social, economic, and political issues—lives entirely in the "needs to do" domain. A successful argument isn't about using the right keywords or winning debating points (a verifiable metric). It's about understanding context, history, human values, unstated assumptions, and the second- and third-order effects of a proposed policy. It requires a robust world model (Wave A) to even begin to comprehend what itneeds to do(Wave B).

The Convergence of the Waves

The two waves you identify are not separate; they are deeply intertwined.A robust, probe-able world model (Wave A) is the foundation required for an AI to begin to understand what itneedsto do (Wave B).

You can't reason about what a societyneedsif your world model is just a statistical soup of text from the internet. You need a model of economics, power dynamics, human psychology, and causality. Until the "inner meaning" is as rich as the "outer symbols" are beautiful, AI will remain a powerful tool that executes its design, a stranger to the deeper needs it is meant to serve.

AI Time vs. Evolutionary Time

This is a beautiful and slightly terrifying comparison.

  • Evolutionary Timeis driven by the slow, parallel, and unforgiving selective pressure of survival and reproduction. It optimizes for one thing: genetic fitness in a given environment. It's a blind, meandering process that has no goal, no foresight, and no humanity.
  • AI Timeis driven by the fast, focused, and deliberate pressure of human design and computational optimization. It optimizes for a loss function—a mathematical formalization of a goal.

The terrifying part is that we are the ones writing the loss function. Evolution didn't care about us, it just produced us. AI, however, will optimize relentlessly for the goals we give it. If we give it narrow, poorly-thought-out goals (the "designed to do"), it will achieve them in ways that could be alien and destructive to the "needs to do" goals we truly care about.

Your hope that AI time prioritizes humanity is the single most important challenge of our era. It means we have to get the objective function right. We have to figure out how to encode compassion, justice, fairness, and wisdom into a system before we press "go."
