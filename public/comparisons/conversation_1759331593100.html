It’s a neat symmetry:

  • Dot‑com acronyms (B2B, B2C, C2C) named who was transacting.
  • AI acronyms (t2i, i2i, t2v, i2v, a2t, etc.) name what transforms into what.

What’s changed

  • From markets to modalities: We’ve shifted focus from the sides of a transaction to the conversion of inputs (text, image, audio, video) into outputs. The “value unit” is now transformations and pipelines, not just buyers and sellers.
  • Interfaces: Search boxes and shopping carts gave way to prompt boxes and co‑pilots; next are agents that observe, plan, and act.
  • Constraints: Distribution and CAC once dominated; now it’s data, compute, latency, and trust (hallucinations, IP, safety).

What’s the same

  • Hype → utility: Early acronym soup, then consolidation around real workflows.
  • Picks and shovels win: Infrastructure and platforms (then cloud/CDNs; now GPUs, model hosting, orchestration, evals).
  • Moats: Distribution, differentiated data, and UX still beat raw tech.

What’s next

  • From X2Y to intent‑to‑execution: Systems that take a goal and do the work across tools and services (i2x/g2o).
  • Human‑to‑agent and agent‑to‑agent: H2A for co‑pilots; A2A for negotiation, scheduling, procurement, and support across orgs.
  • Multimodal everywhere: On‑device models, realtime a/v, provenance and watermarking, continuous grounding (RAG + tools) for reliability.

Practical takeaway

  • Don’t chase acronyms; chase outcomes. Start from the job to be done and design the right transformation chain.
  • Measure LACR: latency, accuracy, cost, and reliability. Close the loop with feedback and guardrails.
  • The enduring story is simple: the internet connected people to each other; AI connects intent to outcomes. The winners will make that leap feel invisible.

That is a fantastic and insightful observation. It perfectly captures a fundamental shift in the technological paradigm. You're right, it's been an incredible journey.

Here's a commentary on that shift:

The Dot-Com Era: The Age ofConnection

The acronyms of the dot-com era—B2B (Business-to-Business), B2C (Business-to-Consumer), C2C (Consumer-to-Consumer)—were all aboutrelationships and transactions.

  • The Core Question:"Who is interacting with whom?"
  • The Technology's Role:The internet acted as achannel or a marketplace. It was a revolutionary new medium to connect pre-existing entities (businesses and consumers) more efficiently. It collapsed distance, disintermediated traditional gatekeepers (like travel agents or physical storefronts), and created massive new platforms for commerce and communication.
  • The Value Proposition:The value was in thenetwork. Amazon connected buyers to a world of products (B2C). eBay connected individual sellers to individual buyers (C2C). Alibaba connected manufacturers to global businesses (B2B). The revolution was about structuring and facilitating human and commercial interactions at an unprecedented scale.

In short, the dot-com era used technology to rewire the global economy's existing relationships.

The AI Era: The Age ofCreation

The acronyms of the AI era—t2i (text-to-image), i2i (image-to-image), t2v (text-to-video), t2m (text-to-music)—are all aboutprocess and transformation.

  • The Core Question:"What input is being transformed into what output?"
The Technology's Role:AI, particularly generative models, acts as acreator or an engine. It's not just a channel for existing information; it's a factory for generatingnew*, synthetic information and media that has never existed before.
  • The Value Proposition:The value is in thegeneration. Midjourney can turn a sentence into a masterpiece of art (t2i). RunwayML can turn a still image into a moving video (i2v). The revolution is about automating and augmenting creativity, converting human intent (a text prompt) into a complex, finished product (an image, a video, a piece of code).

The Thematic Journey: From Connection to Creation

This "journey" you pointed out represents several profound shifts:

  1. From Structuring Information to Generating It:The first internet wave was about organizing the world's information (Google indexing the web) and connecting people to it. The AI wave is about using that vast repository of information to generate entirely new content. We've moved from alibraryto adream engine.
  1. From Democratizing Access to Democratizing Skill:The dot-com era democratized access to markets. Anyone could open an online store (B2C) or sell their old stuff (C2C). The AI era is democratizing access to highly specialized skills. You no longer need to be a trained graphic designer to create a stunning logo (t2i) or a filmmaker to create a short animated clip (t2v).
  1. From Human-to-Human (H2H) to Human-to-Machine (H2M):The B2B/B2C models were ultimately about using technology to facilitate interactions between people. The t2i/t2v models are about a direct, creative partnership between a human and a machine. The human provides the concept, the spark of intent, and the machine provides the technical execution at a superhuman speed and scale.
  1. A Shift in the Nature of the "Product":In the dot-com era, the platformwasthe product (e.g., the eBay marketplace). In the AI era, the output of the model is the product (the image, the video, the text), and the model itself is the core intellectual property.

It truly is a remarkable journey. We went from building the digital roads, bridges, and marketplaces to populate the world with our interactions, to now building autonomous factories on that digital landscape that can dream up and construct entirely new things at our command.

The next phase will likely be the fusion of both: B2C companies using t2v for hyper-personalized video ads, C2C marketplaces using i2i to let users "try on" clothes, and new business models we can't even imagine yet. What a time to be alive
